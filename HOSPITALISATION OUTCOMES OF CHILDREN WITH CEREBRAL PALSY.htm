ABSTRACT

Background: Cerebral palsy (CP) is a debilitating disease that often coexists with multiple comorbidities resulting in frequent and expensive hospitalizations. 

 

Objectives: The study aimed to describe the acute causes of hospitalization, costs, length of stay and associated chronic comorbidities for children living with cerebral palsy (CP) aged 2-13 years admitted at Kenyatta National Hospital (KNH).

 

Methods: A retrospective cross-sectional study of 164 files of children aged 2-13 years living with CP admitted at KNH from January 2017 to December 2021. Files with missing clinical diagnosis, illegible writing and missing data were excluded. Data on sociodemographic characteristics, acute admission diagnosis as indicated on the pediatric admission record (PAR), chronic comorbidities, cost of admission, method of payment and length of stay was abstracted.

 

Results: 82% of the children were urban dwellers with working primary caregivers (80%). The commonest acute admission diagnoses were convulsions (50%) and pneumonia (34%). 32% of the children had respiratory comorbidities, 32% feeding difficulties, 28% seizure disorders, 23% metabolic disorders and 20% malnutrition. The average in-hospital stay was 16.7 days with a median cost of Ksh 46,161, mostly covered by NHIF (71%), while some families paid out-of-pocket, particularly during the first admission. 

 

Conclusion: Seizure and respiratory disorders contributed significantly to both acute and chronic morbidity, with feeding difficulties and malnutrition potentially exacerbating their conditions. Their higher mean hospital stay also increases hospitalization costs. Children with cerebral palsy should be assessed for seizure control, feeding difficulties and nutritional status. Their caregivers should be educated on NHIF insurance during outpatient care. 

 

Key words: Cerebral palsy, Hospitalisation costs, acute morbidity, chronic comorbidity, disease control priorities, health financing

 

 

INTRODUCTION

The term cerebral palsy (CP) describes an umbrella of permanent neurological diseases of posture and movement that limit activity1. The clinical manifestations of CP progress with age and older children often present with severe complications of the disease2. It is also associated with multiple comorbidities including respiratory, neurologic and musculoskeletal illnesses that often require hospitalisation3. Studies done before indicate that children with CP tend to have more hospital admissions and the most common causes of these admissions were found to be respiratory, neurological and gastrointestinal diseases4. Several studies have found that patients with CP have longer in-hospital stays than the general population5 and therefore this translates to an increased risk of in-hospital complications such as infections and deep vein thrombosis as well as poor clinical outcomes6. In addition to this, children with CP often require rehabilitative and surgical interventions from multidisciplinary teams, which come at an added cost, making the medical management of CP highly complex and expensive7. 

 

Limited documentation exists, especially in Africa, regarding CP and other neurological illnesses, with a particular scarcity of information on the health outcomes of children with CP. This knowledge gap can be attributed to various factors, including insufficient funding for research and societal barriers. As a result, understanding the prevalence, causes, and effective interventions for these conditions in Africa is challenging due to the lack of epidemiological data and clinical research. The absence of published studies also hampers the development of tailored strategies and policies to address the specific needs of individuals with CP and other neurological conditions in the region. However, in an effort to bridge this gap, initiatives such as the African Pediatric Neurology Network and collaborative research projects are underway, aiming to enhance knowledge, diagnosis, treatment, and support for affected individuals and their families8,9. These initiatives aim to improve understanding and address the unique challenges faced by individuals with CP and other neurological conditions in Africa, contributing to better healthcare outcomes and quality of life for affected individuals.

 

To further address these concerns, a study was conducted to establish the hospitalization outcomes of children with CP aged 2-13 years admitted at Kenyatta National Hospital (KNH). This study determined common admission diagnoses, comorbidities and average length of stay of children admitted with CP as well as the financial cost of care of these children. We hypothesized that children with CP admitted to KNH have diverse common admission diagnoses, may have comorbidities that contribute to their hospitalization that the average length of stay is expected to vary, and that hospitalization costs may vary and can be a significant financial burden.

 

METHODOLOGY

Study Design and Setting

An institutional-based retrospective cross-sectional study, conducted between 13th March and 9th May 2023 at the Health Records department at KNH in Nairobi, Kenya. 

KNH is a tertiary-care referral and Teaching Hospital with a capacity of 1800 beds. It receives over 60 new CP cases every month. 

Study Population

The population of focus was on the files of in-patient children aged 2-13 years who were admitted to the Paediatric Ward at the KNH from January 2017 to December 2021. All files of children of the stated age, with a clearly documented and confirmed clinical diagnosis of cerebral palsy within the stated five-year period were included in the study. The files with illegible handwriting, those missing clinical diagnoses and files of children with concurrent Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) infection were excluded.

Sample Size and Sampling Procedure

The study estimated the mean length of stay as one of the primary outcome variables. The minimum sample size was calculated using the Fisher's formula, with the assumptions: 95% confidence level, 8.8 standard deviation of the mean length of in-hospital stay (LoS) (Fortin et al., 2021) and absolute error of 1.5. The minimum sample size obtained was 133. From the total population of 643 pediatric patient files generated during the period of study, 341 files were selected at random. From this unbiased sample, those fitting the inclusion criteria were inspected and abstraction was performed from 200 files. 

Data Collection Tool and Procedure

A data abstraction tool was used for data collection. This tool abstracted data on sociodemographic characteristics, acute admission diagnosis, comorbidities, length of in-hospital stay, bill amount and payment methods. Collection of data was carried out by the investigators after ethical approval of the study. The data was obtained from patients' records over the five-year period from January 2017 to December 2021 at the KNH registry.  The information was obtained from Paediatric Admission Records, Clinical Notes, Nursing Cadex, Social Worker Assessment Forms, Discharge Summaries and Billing Reports. 

Operational Definitions and Study Variables

The outcome variables of the study were assessed with the following considerations being held: The acute admission diagnosis was defined as the clinical diagnosis during and within 48 hours of admission. Comorbidities were defined as other illnesses acquired after 48 hours from admission. Length of stay was defined as the duration from admission to discharge. The Financial Cost was defined as costs incurred while offering care to children with cerebral palsy during their stay in hospital. The independent variables assessed in the study were age, sex, occupation residence and NHIF status.

Data Management and Analysis

Personal identifying information was de-identified prior to use in this study. In their place, unique serial codes were assigned to avoid redundancy arising from data replication while still maintaining patient confidentiality. Data quality control measures were employed during the various stages of data handling and processing. This included cleaning and organizing the collected data to ensure that completeness and adequacy of data were met as per the criteria of this study prior to data analysis. Missing and inconsistent data were corrected. 

Data on the occupation of the guardian and residence were further categorized into broad groups for ease of analysis i.e., residence was broadly classified into rural and urban as appropriate while occupation was categorized into employed (formal, informal and self-employed) and unemployed status. Data on the age of participants was organized into categories to facilitate analysis. The number of admissions was capped at a maximum of five for participants with multiple admissions which was further categorized into various groups prior to analysis.

 

Data collected was kept in a password-protected Microsoft Excel spreadsheet and was accessible only to the investigators carrying out the study. The backup files were stored on an external password-protected drive which was in the custody of the principal investigator. 

Data analysis was performed using the Statistical Package for Social Sciences (SPSS) version 21 to draw inferences from which conclusions were made on the research objectives. Descriptive and inferential statistical tests were performed on the data collected including socio-demographic and outcome variables of interest i.e., age, sex, residence, occupational status, acute admission diagnosis, comorbidities, average LoS and financial cost. Continuous variables were summarized using means, medians and modes while their dispersion were expressed as interquartile ranges (IQR), variances and standard deviations. Categorical variables were summarized using appropriate frequencies and proportions. The data was analyzed at the individual admission level and stratified by the number of admissions. Analyzed data was presented in the form of relevant figures and tables.

Ethical Issues

Approval to conduct research was sought from the Kenyatta National Hospital. Ethical clearance and waiver of informed consent from the Kenyatta National Hospital-University of Nairobi Ethics and Research Committee was also sought. Confidentiality of patient data was maintained through deidentification and strict access protocols by the investigators.

 

 

RESULTS

Screening and Enrollment Process

643 files belonging to pediatric patients with CP and admitted to KNH over the period of the study were identified from the registry. Of these, 302 were excluded for not meeting the inclusion criteria for this study. The remaining 341 files were received for sampling and from these, 200 files were selected at random for data collection. Abstraction was performed on those 200 files. 36 files were excluded from these for missing key outcome data resulting in a final 164-file data being cleaned and analyzed for the study. (See Figure 1)

Sociodemographic Characteristics of Study Population

Out of 164 participants, 84 (51%) were male and 80 (48.8%) were female. The occupations of the caregivers to the children admitted comprised majorly of informal employment with 70(42.7%) while 42(25.6%) and 22(13.4%) had self and formal employment respectively. 29(17.7%) of the caregivers of the participants were unemployed. For their residence, 135(82.3%) of participants reside in urban settings and 29(17.7%) reside in rural areas. The mean age of the participants was 5.1 years with a standard deviation of 2.9. (See Table 1)

The Common Acute Admission Diagnoses of Children with CP Admitted at KNH

The acute diagnoses made on admission of these children with CP were identified as shown in Table 2. Some of the patients had more than one diagnosis on admission and these were all included in establishing the common admission diagnoses. Convulsive Disorders were the most common cause of admission having been noted in 82 admissions of the 164 analyzed in this study (50.0%). Pneumonia was the second most common admission diagnosis with 55 diagnoses (33.5%) of the entity being made. These were then followed by other gastrointestinal disorders (11.6%) such as vomiting, other respiratory disorders (10.4%) aside from pneumonia, and malnutrition (10.4%). 9.8% had acute gastroenteritis and 9.1% had gastroesophageal reflux disease while 8.5% had febrile illnesses and 7.9% had adenoid/tonsillar hypertrophy. Of the participant files analyzed, 7.3% were diagnosed with meningitis/meningoencephalitis and those diagnosed with fractures and urinary tract infections made up 4.3% and 0.6% respectively. (See Table 2)

 

The Reported Comorbidities of Children with CP Admitted at KNH

Comorbidities were identified from the files obtained and recorded as shown in table 3. The top comorbidity noted in the children with CP during their admission was respiratory disorders, being present in 53 files. This made up 32.3% of the 164 analyzed files. Eating difficulties followed as the second most common comorbidity with 52 files (31.7%) noting these difficulties. Following these were seizure disorders (28.0%), metabolic disorders (23.2%), and malnutrition (20.1%) consecutively. Various other comorbidities were identified: for 28(17.1%), gastrointestinal disturbances; for 24(14.6%), blood disorders; for 14(8.5%), speech impairment; for 6(3.7%), visual impairment; for 4(2.4%), sleep disorders; for another 4(2.4%), hearing disabilities; for 3(1.8%), thyroid dysfunction; and for 1(0.6%) each, cognitive impairment, scoliosis, and mental disorders.   (See Table 3)

The average length of in-hospital stay in days of children admitted with CP at KNH

Length of in-hospital stay varied among the admissions analyzed. The longest admission duration lasted 139 days while the shortest admission was 1 day long. The average length of in-hospital stay was found to be 16.7 days, with the median stay being 11 days. (See Figure 2) 

 

The Financial Cost of Hospital Admission of Children Admitted With Cerebral Palsy

The financial cost of hospital admission of children admitted was found to be varied among the various admissions. The average financial cost of admission was found to be KES 80,829.47, with the maximum financial cost reported found to be KES 787,503 and the lowest reported was found to be KES 2,265. Majority of the financial cost reported was found to be covered by NHIF (116). Out-of-pocket payments (37) and other insurances (4) were also reported to cover the financial cost incurred. (See Figure 3)

 

DISCUSSION

In this study, we analyzed the most common causes of admission for children with cerebral palsy. The primary reasons were convulsive disorder and pneumonia. Other acute diagnoses included gastrointestinal disorders, malnutrition, respiratory disorders, acute gastroenteritis, gastroesophageal reflux disease, febrile illness, adenoid/tonsillar hypertrophy, meningitis, fractures, and urinary tract infections. The most frequently reported comorbidities were respiratory diseases, eating difficulties, seizure disorders, metabolic disorders, and malnutrition. Cerebral palsy patients had considerably longer lengths of stay and incurred high costs associated with the care and treatment of associated diseases and recurrent admissions.

 

The study findings were consistent with other studies4,5,10,11 which established common causes of admission and associated comorbidities among children with CP. The common causes of admission corresponded to those established by Young et al. (2011)11, namely convulsive disorders and pneumonia. Gladstone (2010)12 and Young et al. (2011)11 reported that these were the most common reasons for frequent readmission among children with cerebral palsy. The associated comorbidities, including respiratory diseases, eating difficulties, seizure disorders, metabolic disorders, and malnutrition, were consistent with the findings by Duke et al. (2021)13.

 

Cerebral palsy is associated with a longer length of stay. Our findings established an average length of stay for cerebral palsy patients to be 16.7 days, as established by Fortin et al. (2021)14. In comparison to the study by Chiang et al. (2019)5, the average length of stay was lower than the reported average length of stay of 59 days. There is a high cost associated with the care of children with CP, as reported by Ismail A et al. (2022)15. While the majority of the cost was covered by insurance providers, some families had to pay out-of-pocket for their children's care, particularly during their first admission.

 

Efficient multidisciplinary teams with sufficient medical resources should be established to manage and care for patients with cerebral palsy. This would improve their quality of life, reduce the frequency of admission and length of stay, and decrease associated hospitalization costs. We advocate for improved data recording among healthcare professionals caring for children with cerebral palsy, teaching caregivers first aid management of actively convulsing children, routine vaccination for children to reduce morbidity arising from pneumonia infections, and frequent nutritional assessment and developmental monitoring, as well as offering advice and support to caregivers.

 

This study had a number of limitations. Firstly, it only focused on pediatric cerebral palsy patients aged 2-13 years and does not provide insights on older patients. Secondly, the study's accuracy heavily relied on the quality of the recorded information from which data was collected.

 

CONCLUSION

This is the first study in Kenya to investigate the hospitalization outcomes of children admitted with cerebral palsy and will provide seminal information for current practice in medical care and future studies for children with cerebral palsy in Kenya. We recommended further studies on older children with cerebral palsy in order to establish their hospitalizations outcome and associated trends.
